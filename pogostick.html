Physics Tech Demo · 2025

Pogo Controller

A spring-based pogo controller built to feel physically real while staying predictable enough to design levels around.

RoleSolo Developer & Technical Game Designer

EngineUnreal Engine 5 (Blueprints)

  • Physics Simulation
  • Custom Impulse
  • Blueprint Scripting

Unreal Engine 5 (Blueprints)

Project Overview

This project is a gameplay physics prototype focused on building a physics-driven pogo controller. Rather than creating a complete game, the goal was to explore how a spring-based movement system could feel both physically believable and responsive enough for gameplay.

The core design goal was to create movement that players could learn and predict. Every landing is a commitment, so players should be able to understand how their landing choice influences their next bounce. To achieve this, I built a hybrid system that combines physics simulation with gameplay logic, allowing the controller to feel physical while remaining consistent and predictable.

Physics Architecture

The controller is divided into two responsibilities: physics simulation and gameplay logic.

The physics simulation handles spring compression, collisions, gravity, friction, and naturally preserves the player's momentum. Gameplay logic handles everything the player controls, including rotating the pogo stick and generating the bounce after landing.

When the pogo lands, the spring compresses naturally through the physics simulation. Once the spring reaches maximum compression, the gameplay system cancels the spring's rebound and replaces it with a custom impulse. Instead of resetting the player's movement, the underlying physics simulation continues handling momentum and collision response while gameplay only takes control at the moment of launch.

This separation allows the movement to remain physically believable while ensuring the bounce reflects the player's landing choice rather than the unpredictable behavior of the spring simulation.

Engineering Challenges

Separating Physics from Gameplay

One of the biggest design challenges was deciding where physics should stop and gameplay should begin.

Physics determines how the player arrives at each landing through momentum, collisions, gravity, friction, and spring compression. Gameplay expresses the player's intent by controlling the orientation of the pogo stick and generating the custom bounce.

One problem I ran into was how the player controlled the landing orientation. My first approach rotated the bottom contact point of the pogo directly. Since the top and bottom of the pogo are connected through the same physics constraint, rotating the contact point also forced the top of the pogo to move. Instead of responding naturally, the physics simulation was constantly fighting against the player input, making the movement feel unstable.

To fix this, I stopped rotating the contact point directly and instead converted the player's input into angular forces. Those forces are applied through the physics constraint, allowing the simulation to control how the entire pogo rotates. The player still controls the landing orientation, but the movement now works with the physics simulation instead of against it.

Keeping these responsibilities separate made the controller easier to tune while ensuring the player's landing choice remained the primary factor in predicting the next bounce.

Consistent Bounce Generation

Unlike a traditional platformer, the player never directly controls when or how high they jump. Every jump is generated by the pogo stick after landing.

My initial implementation relied entirely on the spring constraint to generate each bounce. While this produced physically accurate behavior, every consecutive bounce naturally lost energy through the simulation. Over time, each launch became weaker until the pogo no longer generated enough height to sustain traversal. This made movement inconsistent and difficult to design levels around.

To solve this, I separated the spring's compression from the player's launch. The physics simulation is responsible for compressing the spring and handling collisions, while the gameplay system generates a custom impulse once maximum compression has been reached. This allows the spring to retain its physical appearance and behavior while ensuring every valid bounce produces a consistent launch. The result is movement that remains physically believable without allowing the spring simulation to gradually lose the energy required for gameplay.

Prototype Outcome

This prototype established the core architecture for a physics-based pogo controller that balances physical simulation with player control. Rather than pursuing a perfectly realistic pogo simulation, the system prioritizes predictable movement so players can build an understanding of how their landing choices affect future trajectories.

Although the surrounding gameplay is still in development, the controller provides a strong technical foundation for future momentum-based mechanics.

Future Improvements

One improvement I would explore is making the bounce respond to both the player's landing alignment and the momentum carried into the landing, rather than using the same impulse every time.

Because the controller is physics-driven, the player's incoming momentum naturally comes from the simulation. The player can influence that momentum through landing placement, previous bounces, and friction, but the gameplay system controls the outgoing bounce.

In a future iteration, I would combine both pieces of information to determine the strength of the launch. Well-aligned landings with strong incoming momentum would generate the strongest bounce, while poor landings or low momentum would produce weaker launches. This would reward good movement and positioning while preserving the physical nature of the controller and strengthening the connection between player skill and momentum.
