Sonara

A rhythm game where the world performs the music with you.

Sonara is a rhythm game built solo in Unreal Engine 5. A ball travels along a branching path, steered left and right to land on circles synced to a song. What makes it different from most rhythm games is that the environment doesn't just sit there — rocks, trees, and props react to the same beat the player does, so the world feels like it's performing the song alongside you, not just providing a backdrop for it.

The footage shown is grey box — no environment art yet. I prioritized getting the underlying systems exactly right before spending time on visuals that would need to be rebuilt if those systems changed.

Unreal Engine 5 · C++ / Blueprint

How it works

The player steers a ball left and right. Circles appear ahead, placed at exact timestamps pulled from the song's timeline, and the ball automatically arcs toward whichever one is next — landing on it exactly on the beat. Partway through, the path forks into two routes with different rhythmic identities, then merges back together before the song's most intense section.

Why I built it this way

Why a hardware-locked audio clock instead of a free-running timer

Early versions used a simple timer to track musical time. Over a 45-second song, even a millisecond of drift per beat accumulates into noticeable desync — the ball stops landing on the beat by the end of the track. I rebuilt the timing system around Unreal's Quartz subsystem, which locks to the actual audio hardware rather than counting time independently. The clock and the song are now mathematically guaranteed to stay in sync regardless of song length.

Why timestamps instead of beat numbers

My first approach placed circles at calculated beat numbers (beat 1, beat 2, beat 3...) assuming a perfectly steady tempo. Real recorded music isn't perfectly metronomic — a live drummer pushes and pulls slightly even on a click track. I switched to placing circles at exact timestamps taken directly from analyzing the song's waveform in Audacity, so each circle lands exactly where the music actually hits, not where math says it theoretically should.

Why a modular spline architecture

A single spline can't support branching paths — there's no way for one curve to represent two different routes through the same section of music. I built a system where each section of the path is its own independent spline object, holding its own circle data. The ball can switch between sections at runtime, including reassigning which section it "came from" dynamically — which became necessary once paths could merge back together from two different directions.

Why one reusable actor drives all environment choreography

Rather than hand-animating individual props, I built a single reusable actor (internally called BP_BounceProp) that listens to the same beat clock the ball does. Any prop — a rock, a tree, a piece of foliage — can be dropped into the level and given this behavior, with its own height, rotation range, and timing offset. A global intensity value scales every instance at once, so the whole environment can escalate together during a song's most intense moment without touching each prop individually.

Problems I had to debug

Problem

The double-fire

For a while, hit detection and the score system were firing twice per beat — sometimes consistently, sometimes only on certain circles. I tried several fixes at the flag level in C++, and each one either didn't work or broke something else (the ball would freeze after two beats, or stop syncing on fast sections).

The actual cause turned out to be a re-entrancy problem: when the beat event fired, the Blueprint logic that responded to it would set up the next beat's timestamp while still inside the same function call that had just fired the event. That reset a "has this fired" flag before the original call had fully finished, which let a second, overlapping check pass and fire the event again in the same frame. The real fix was removing a small timing tolerance window that, combined with that reset, created a brief window where the condition could pass twice. Once removed, the event fires exactly once per beat, every time.

Problem

Bounce placement inconsistency

The ball started landing visibly behind circles. I initially suspected timestamp precision, then circle world positions, then audio subdivision granularity. None of those held up under testing.

The actual cause was when making a spline and stretching it out, if you didnt add points in between the beginning and end of the spline, circle world space positions relative to their position along the spline skews. This was noticable on flatter level sections. The other sections were going uphill and the problem here was the circles were placed too far away from the actual spline center making relative distance along spline also be skewed. Restoring proportional spacing by adding spline points to a stretched spline and keeping circles closer to the spline center when elevation is used fixed it immediately.

Problem

Branching paths merge correctly

When two paths merged back into one shared section, the camera transition was breaking. It was breaking because the merge section stored a reference to which path it came from, and that reference was set at edit time to one path. If I took the other fork path, the camera would snap to the path that was assigned which was hard locked. To fix this I needed to dynamically assign the path in which the ball came from live, so right before the ball switches into the merge section, it stamps the merge section's PreviousSection with wherever it actually just came from.

Small change in the end, but it only became obvious once I stopped thinking about it as a wiring problem and started thinking about it as a timing problem. The information existed; it was just being captured at the wrong moment.

A decision I reversed

At one point I considered making the ball's bounce direction follow the path's banking angle, so it would visually launch perpendicular to a tilted circle's surface instead of always straight up. It looked great on paper, but if the path ever curved past a certain bank angle, the same logic would have caused the ball to bounce in a physically wrong direction relative to the player's expectations.

I reverted to a simpler world-relative bounce and accepted a minor visual inconsistency on sharply banked sections, rather than risk a system that could break the core feel of the game under specific path geometry. I've noted a cleaner version of this for later — reading bounce direction from each circle's own rotation instead of the path's banking — but it wasn't worth the risk for the current build.

What's not built yet, on purpose

Environment art, VFX, additional choreography (water, wildlife, lighting), and a start/end statistics screen are all deliberately not in this build. The systems above were the part that needed to be correct before any of that work could be trusted not to need redoing.
